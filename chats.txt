Real World Data in Pharma: Current Trends & Application Development Analysis
Introduction
Real World Data (RWD) is fundamentally reshaping how the pharmaceutical industry generates evidence, conducts research, and obtains regulatory approval. The pharmaceutical industry is under constant pressure to innovate — developing effective treatments that improve patient outcomes while navigating the challenges of cost, time, and regulatory scrutiny. Traditionally, drug development follows a linear process heavily reliant on tightly controlled clinical trials, which often fail to capture the nuances of real-world patient experiences. This is exactly why the integration of RWD and its transformation into real-world evidence (RWE) is revolutionizing the healthcare industry, enabling faster improvements to treatments and uncovering new opportunities, such as off-label uses.​1​In 2025, 75% of pharmaceutical firms reported using RWD in drug development, and over half were actively integrating AI tools with RWD to accelerate insights.​2​ This report breaks down the current state of RWD across five key themes, followed by a strategic recommendation for the best application to develop.

Theme 1: Regulatory Acceleration — The Policy Tailwind
The Global Regulatory Push
The most consequential trend in RWD is the accelerating embrace by global regulators who are formalizing its role in decision-making.

In December 2024, FDA's Center for Drug Evaluation and Research announced a new Center for Real-World Evidence Innovation that will coordinate and promote the use of RWD in regulatory decision-making. The agency also published a number of guidance documents on data in 2023 and 2024.​3​By early 2026, the FDA had significantly expanded its RWE program, finalizing updated guidance on real-world evidence for medical devices (December 2025) that allows sponsors to use aggregate or de-identified data rather than requiring identifiable patient-level records. The agency also launched FDA-RWE-ACCELERATE, its first agency-wide initiative to advance RWE integration across all product centers.​2​

The European Front
In Europe, the progress has been equally significant. The EMA's DARWIN EU® network expanded from 20 to 30 data partners across 16 countries, covering approximately 180 million patients. A total of 59 studies were conducted — representing a 47.5% increase from the previous year. The median duration for DARWIN EU® studies was just four months from protocol approval to final study results — much faster than traditional RWE approaches — making them suited for timely integration into regulatory timelines.​4​The European Health Data Space (EHDS) regulation, which entered into force in March 2025, further positions Europe for large-scale RWD integration.​2​

Regulatory Context by Use Case
The FDA defines RWD as data relating to patient health status and/or the delivery of health care routinely collected from a variety of sources, including electronic health records, medical claims data, product or disease registries, and data gathered from digital health technologies. RWE is the clinical evidence about the usage and potential benefits or risks of a medical product derived from the analysis of RWD. The 21st Century Cures Act of 2016 was specifically designed to accelerate medical product development and bring new innovations faster to patients.​5​

Theme 2: Data Sources and Quality — The Foundational Challenge
What Constitutes RWD?
Real-world data encompasses health information collected outside the confines of clinical trials, including: Electronic Health Records (EHRs), Claims and Billing Data, Patient Registries, Wearable and Mobile Device Data, Patient-Reported Outcomes, and Patient-Generated Data. When RWD is analyzed using robust methodologies, it transforms into real-world evidence (RWE), providing actionable insights that demonstrate the safety, efficacy, and value of treatments in real-world settings.​1​

The Quality Problem
Data quality remains the Achilles' heel of RWD. Most healthcare data sets are more suited to administrative and billing purposes than research and RWE. As such, they are often incomplete and may include built-in biases. Visibility into RWD is often limited until after the data set is purchased, which makes a comprehensive appraisal of the data challenging, time-consuming, and potentially expensive. The depth, breadth, coverage, timeliness, and potential impact of data are all critical dimensions that need to be considered in quality assessments.​6​McKinsey estimated that over the next three to five years, an average top-20 pharmaceutical company could unlock more than $300 million a year by adopting RWE across its value chain — but this potential is contingent on addressing the fundamental data quality gaps that limit current RWD applications.​6​

AI as a Quality Enabler
In one study of heart failure patients, an AI-based NLP approach achieved an F1 accuracy of 94.1% in phenotyping key clinical concepts from electronic records, versus only 49.0% using traditional (manual) methods. In 2025, Flatiron Health demonstrated that LLMs can match expert human abstractors in extracting real-world cancer progression events across 14 cancer types, introducing the VALID Framework — the industry's first comprehensive approach to evaluating AI-extracted RWD quality.​2​

Theme 3: Key Applications — Where RWD Is Making an Impact
3a. External Control Arms (ECAs)
External control arms are becoming more mainstream in clinical research, supported by FDA guidance that facilitates leveraging real-world data and ECAs in the overall drug discovery process. This serves as "an incredible source to help improve efficiency and the quality of research overall."​3​External control arms may be sourced from prior clinical trial data (individual or pooled), or observational, real-world data such as registries, electronic health records, and medical or pharmacy claims — offering significant cost and time savings versus running full parallel control groups in trials.​7​

3b. Pharmacovigilance and Safety Signal Detection
AI in pharmacovigilance is a particularly fertile ground due to several factors: procedures are elaborate, mature, and high-sensitivity (good fit for automation); multiple information flows are required including scientific literature and regulatory documentation; and ICSRs (Individual Case Safety Reports) carry a wealth of unstructured information. Likely new methodologies will extend from rule-based algorithms to data-driven AI/ML algorithms and harvest the power of Large Language Models to improve insights and streamline information processing.​8​AI applied to real-world data (e.g., electronic health care records) has been identified as a potentially promising technical paradigm for the pharmacovigilance field, with instances of AI approaches applied to both structured and unstructured RWD (conducting natural language processing on clinical notes and other sources).​9​

3c. Drug Development Acceleration
RWD and RWE are game-changers for pharmaceutical innovation across the entire drug development pipeline. In Target Identification: RWD enables researchers to analyze large-scale datasets to detect patterns in disease progression and identify subpopulations with unique genetic or environmental factors. In Clinical Trial Design: RWD helps identify eligible patients quickly, guides the selection of meaningful endpoints, and enables adaptive trials with real-time data analysis. In Post-Market Surveillance: early detection of adverse effects in diverse populations, discovery of off-label uses, and iterative improvements to dosages and formulations.​1​A pharmaceutical company using RWD for oncology trials can bring a drug to market six months ahead of schedule, achieving an estimated $500 million in additional revenue, supported by strong RWE that satisfied regulatory agencies and payers.​1​

3d. Predictive Analytics and AI Integration
Predictive analytics and AI can help predict disease progression and assist with early detection to support patient recruitment and retention. These technologies can identify trends for data and lead to informed decisions across the drug life cycle.​3​

Theme 4: Privacy, Interoperability, and Data Governance
The Privacy Imperative
With the growing adoption of RWE for regulatory and healthcare decision-making, data privacy has become an even more critical consideration. In the United States, HIPAA was passed in 1996 to create standards that protect the privacy of identifiable health information. In Europe, GDPR was put into effect in 2018 to ensure the fundamental rights of data protection and strengthen oversight over the collection, sharing, and use of personal information. Additionally, Canada, Singapore, India, and China have also recently published similar data privacy laws to protect personal health information.​10​

Privacy-Preserving Technologies
Synthetic data and federated networks provide promising venues to access real-world healthcare data in a privacy-preserving manner for evidence generation. Synthetic health data are artificial data that mimic the properties and relationships seen in real patient data, preserving statistical properties without being acquired from actual patients. Federated data networks allow identifiable data to remain under the control of its original stewards, behind their firewall, but enable users to run analyses across multiple sites without centralizing or aggregating the data.​10​Regulatory policies and privacy rules (HIPAA, GDPR, California's CCPA) impose constraints on data use, driving the need for privacy-enhancing techniques such as synthetic data generation and federated learning.​2​

Interoperability Challenges
Acquiring RWD is challenged by friction in data acquisition — specifically, data regulations and the effort required for care providers to securely and effectively share sensitive data. Data silos are a major barrier: RWD often resides in fragmented systems, making collaboration difficult. Bias and quality issues, where incomplete or inconsistent data can skew results, compound the challenge.​1​

Theme 5: Conflicting Viewpoints and Research Uncertainties
The Bias and Validity Debate
RWD is inherently noisy, sparse, and heterogeneous, and AI methods can amplify biases if used naively. Quality issues such as missing data, inconsistent coding, and limited documentation require careful preprocessing. Expert consensus stresses that "special precautions" are needed when applying AI to RWD: validation on real-world cohorts, transparency of algorithms, and incorporation of medical domain knowledge are essential to avoid "potentially harmful fallacies."​2​

Regulatory Acceptance Is Uneven
RWD acceptance is context-dependent and heterogeneous across countries, with the UK remaining more receptive to consider RWD, Italy relying on RWD to inform managed-entry agreements, and France endorsing RWD to support technology re-evaluations — while in some markets expert interview revealed that mature trial data still weighs more than RWD.​11​

The Gold Standard Debate
Real-world evidence is becoming increasingly important in the regulation of medicines, offering valuable insights particularly in post-marketing surveillance and in situations where randomized controlled trials (RCTs) are not feasible. While clinical trials remain the gold standard, RWE complements them by providing additional evidence that can enhance decision-making.​12​ The central tension — whether RWE can replace rather than merely supplement RCTs in certain contexts — remains an active area of methodological and regulatory debate.

1) Macro trend: RWD is shifting from “nice-to-have” to decision‑grade evidence—but only if it’s fit‑for‑purpose
Over the last ~2 years, regulators have made the direction unmistakable: RWD can support regulatory decision‑making, but quality, transparency, and methodological rigor are the gating factors. The FDA’s guidance explicitly frames RWD/RWE as part of an ongoing program to support decisions such as new indications for already‑approved drugs and post‑approval requirements.  At the same time, industry observers note “external control arms” and AI‑enabled analytics becoming more mainstream, largely because regulators have clarified expectations around using EHR/claims data. Why this matters: The competitive advantage is no longer merely having data—it’s being able to prove that your data + methods yield credible causal or comparative insight under audit and scrutiny. 2) Theme A — Regulatory & policy pull: formal networks, guidance, and new decision points
A1. Europe: Federated RWE at scale (DARWIN EU) + new HTA gates (JCA)
Europe has moved beyond pilots into infrastructure. DARWIN EU is positioned as a standing capability that provides evidence on use, safety, effectiveness, using real‑world healthcare databases and a federated model; its public “key figures” cite ~250M patients (Europe, 2026) and ~110 studies delivered since 2022, with data standardized to OMOP for faster cross‑site analyses.  Complementing this, the EU’s Joint Clinical Assessment (JCA) process became in‑scope for new cancer medicines and ATMPs from 12 Jan 2025, creating a new, centralized evidence “moment” that manufacturers must plan for. [ema.europa.eu], [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov] [health.ec.europa.eu], [iconplc.com]
Conflict / uncertainty: While EU HTA aims to harmonize clinical assessment, multiple sources highlight that how RWE will be treated is still inconsistent or unclear, especially where single‑arm evidence is common (rare disease/oncology). A 2024 paper argues early guidance risks being “idealistic” by down‑weighting RWE/external controls—potentially worsening access inequities. [ncbi.nlm.nih.gov], [ispor.org]
A2. US: FDA’s RWE program is explicit—data suitability and study design are the battlefields
The FDA’s 2023 guidance situates RWD/RWE in a formal program created under the 21st Century Cures Act, and the FDA continues to publish/refresh RWE materials describing where RWE fits across the product lifecycle.  Additionally, FDA’s safety ecosystem continues to mature: the Sentinel Initiative reports activity and accomplishments for 2022–2024, underscoring that large distributed networks are now routine for post‑market assessment. [fda.gov], [fda.gov] [sentinelin...iative.org], [pubmed.ncb...lm.nih.gov]
Practical implication: Sponsors increasingly must design RWE studies as if they’ll face “trial‑like” scrutiny—prespecification, lineage, reproducibility, and sensitivity analyses are no longer optional. NICE echoes this direction: its framework updates include added focus on bias/external validity and tools supporting target trial approaches and reporting derived-from-unstructured elements. [nice.org.uk], [nice.org.uk]

3) Theme B — Data modality expansion: from claims/EHR to linked, multimodal “patient truth”
B1. The center of gravity is shifting toward linked datasets (claims ↔ EHR ↔ labs ↔ notes)
The highest‑value RWE increasingly comes from linkage, because claims alone often tell you “what happened” (fills, procedures) but not “why,” while EHR notes/labs add clinical context. Internally, 2025 RWD Data Exploration References frames exactly this trade‑space:

Closed claims: high capture, longitudinal, but latency ~3 months and less payer/account detail;
Open claims: higher volume, lower latency (~1 week), but fragmented and noisier;
EMR: richer clinical detail + notes, but lower coverage and heavier analytics burden;
Lab: biomarker and testing pathways, but incomplete coverage and vendor gaps. [2025 RWD D...References | PowerPoint], [biopharmin...tional.com]

The same internal deck explicitly describes “emergent properties” when linked (e.g., EMR + claims adds ‘why’ to ‘how much’; labs + claims support cost‑effectiveness and outcomes‑based contracting), which mirrors broader industry movement toward evidence built from multi‑source, cross‑validated signals. [2025 RWD D...References | PowerPoint], [pubmed.ncb...lm.nih.gov]
B2. Public infrastructure is converging on common data models + query‑ready networks
On the FDA side, a 2024 publication describes Sentinel’s Real‑World Evidence Data Enterprise (RWE‑DE) as a distributed network designed to expand regulatory‑grade RWD utility, including EHR‑claims linked assets and standardized processes (including handling free‑text notes for retrieval).  In Europe, DARWIN EU emphasizes standardization into OMOP so data can be analyzed faster and more consistently across partners. [pubmed.ncb...lm.nih.gov], [sentinelin...iative.org] [ema.europa.eu], [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov]
Conflict / uncertainty: Common data models improve scale and reproducibility, but mapping is imperfect. A 2024 OMOP implementation report highlights that outcomes, social determinants, devices, and specific treatments were harder to characterize, and even sites on the same EHR can implement OMOP differently—creating hidden heterogeneity. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov], [ema.europa.eu]

4) Theme C — Methods maturation: target trial emulation, external controls, and AI/NLP for messy reality
C1. Target trial emulation is becoming a “default language” for credible observational inference
HTA bodies and evidence evaluators increasingly push RWE toward prespecified causal questions and explicit bias handling. NICE’s framework updates explicitly add references and tools to support target trial approaches, and expand discussion of external validity bias and adjustment for differences between study sample and target population.  This aligns with the broader regulatory narrative: RWE is acceptable when the research question, data suitability, and analysis plan are built to minimize bias and characterize uncertainty. [nice.org.uk], [nice.org.uk] [fda.gov], [fda.gov]
C2. External control arms (ECAs) are rising—but remain contentious
Industry commentary points to ECAs becoming more mainstream in clinical research and a driver of efficiency.  Yet the controversy is real: EU HTA stakeholders warn that down‑weighting RWE/external controls could harm rare disease access, while others worry that over‑accepting ECAs risks systematic bias if comparability and confounding aren’t convincingly addressed. [biopharmin...tional.com], [veranahealth.com] [ncbi.nlm.nih.gov], [nice.org.uk]
Where uncertainty lives:

Unmeasured confounding (severity, functional status, socioeconomic factors)
Time‑zero alignment and immortal time bias
Endpoint validity in routine care (coding vs clinical truth)
These concerns are exactly why HTA frameworks emphasize transparency, validation, and sensitivity analyses rather than “black‑box” results. [nice.org.uk], [nice.org.uk]

C3. Unstructured data is the next frontier (notes, ePRO, devices)—but validation is the price of admission
The most valuable signals increasingly sit in free text and patient‑reported streams. NICE explicitly updated sections on methods using unstructured data and the need to validate how structured elements are derived.  Internally, 2025 RWD Data Exploration References highlights unstructured HCP notes as a differentiator (“why behind prescribing”), while 2026 REGN RWE Value Ad Hoc Support 2.6.26 includes examples where patient‑reported symptom rates differentiated therapies that appeared clinically similar—illustrating why real‑world PROs are gaining traction. [nice.org.uk], [nice.org.uk] [2025 RWD D...References | PowerPoint], [2026 REGN...ort 2.6.26 | PowerPoint]

5) Theme D — Operationalization: data quality systems, governance, and “evidence ops” become core capabilities
As RWE becomes decision‑grade, organizations are building operational delivery models (automation, QA, SOPs) around RWD pipelines. Conflict / uncertainty: Federated networks (DARWIN EU, Sentinel) reduce data movement and improve privacy posture, but they can slow exploratory iteration and complicate “single source of truth” governance. Meanwhile, centralized warehousing can speed iteration but raises privacy/security risk and contracting friction. These are not solved problems; they’re trade‑offs that vary by use case and jurisdiction. [ema.europa.eu], [nice.org.uk]
